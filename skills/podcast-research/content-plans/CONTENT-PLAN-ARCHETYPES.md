# Content Plan Archetypes — Publishing Calendar Guide

Run `workflows/content-plan.md` to generate the full calendar for any episode.
This file tells you **how the calendar should differ by guest archetype** —
which seed types will be strongest, which angles to push, and what not to write.

Named examples are real guests who represent each type.

---

## How This File Works

Every content plan starts the same way: mine the completed brief for the five
seed types (Macro Hook, Data Story, Counternarrative, Episode Frame, Long-Form).
What changes by archetype is which seeds will be strongest, which will be weak,
and what the common calendar mistakes look like for each guest type.

---

## Archetype 1 — TradFi Institutional
*e.g., Robbie Mitchnick (BlackRock)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — TradFi guests generate the best "actually..." posts
  because their domain (ETF mechanics, custody, RWA) is full of claims CT
  repeats without understanding the plumbing. The authorized participant
  mechanism, in-kind creation, custody concentration — all high-engagement
  correction territory.
- **Data Story** — Institutional adoption is full of quantifiable claims.
  ETF AUM, custody concentration percentages, Coinbase's ~70% share of BTC
  ETF custody, BUIDL AUM milestones. These land well when stated plainly
  against what CT narratives claim.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Macro Hook** — TradFi guests are often the macro hook themselves. Don't
  make a separate macro-hook post about macro conditions that sets up the
  episode; they have enough profile that the pre-drop thread can carry the
  frame. The macro context belongs inside the pre-drop thread, not as a
  standalone post.

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative tweet — correct the ETF/custody/RWA claim CT is getting wrong
  → Data story — one clean institutional adoption number + one implication

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — "why BlackRock's [role], why now" — lead with
    the structural unlock (regulatory, product launch, market event), not the person

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — focus on the one thing they said that contradicts
    the institutional narrative (these guests always slip something candid)
  → Quotable standalone — the most technically precise thing they said about
    how the plumbing actually works

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: "What an ETF authorized participant actually does" or
    "The difference between a trust charter and a bank license"
```

**What NOT to write:**
- A piece that frames institutional adoption as purely positive. These guests
  often have the most interesting things to say about why TradFi is slow —
  not why it's fast. The interesting content is the friction, not the celebration.
- A generic "BlackRock validates crypto" take. Every publication will write
  this. Your counternarrative version of that take is the one worth writing.

## Archetype 2 — Market Maker / Trading Firm
*e.g., Jake Ostrovskis (Wintermute)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Data Story** — Market making guests sit on proprietary views of spread
  dynamics, volume distribution, and execution quality. The data stories
  here are often things CT doesn't know: which pairs have real depth vs.
  thin-book manipulation risk, how on-chain volume compares to CEX at
  specific trade sizes, where private orderflow is concentrating.
- **Counternarrative** — These guests are some of the most effective
  generators of "liquidity isn't what you think it is" takes. TVL and
  volume numbers that include wash trading or thin-book depth are correctable
  with their actual market view.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Long-form article** — Market structure content ages fast because the
  venues and mechanics change. Write the long-form piece only if the
  mechanism is durable (e.g., "how funding rate works" or "what PBS
  actually does") — not if it's about current venue rankings.

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Data story — the spread/volume/funding rate data point that contradicts
    a narrative claim circulating on CT right now
  → Counternarrative — one "liquidity" or "volume" claim that's technically
    wrong using their domain knowledge as the source

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with a market structure moment (specific
    liquidation cascade, perp funding spike, MEV event) as the frame,
    then introduce the guest as the person who was inside it

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — the mechanism reveal: what did they explain
    about how market structure actually works that most people don't know?
  → Quotable standalone — their most precise technical claim about execution

EVERGREEN:
  → Article only if the mechanism is durable. Good candidates:
    "What private orderflow concentration means for retail execution quality"
    "Why funding rate is a sentiment signal but not a hard peg"
```

**What NOT to write:**
- A piece centered on price targets or directional calls. That's not
  what makes these guests valuable.
- A "meet Wintermute / Jake O" intro piece. CT knows who the major market
  makers are. Skip the credential-establishment post and get to the mechanism.

## Archetype 3 — Crypto-Native VC / Analyst
*e.g., Haseeb Qureshi (Dragonfly), Sean Lippel (Framework Ventures)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — VCs make public theses. When those theses have been
  stress-tested by the market, the counternarrative writes itself. For Haseeb
  specifically: his published writing on L1 fee compression, modular vs.
  monolithic chain dynamics, and DeFi protocol revenue are public record and
  directly correctable with current data.
- **Episode Frame (pre-drop thread)** — VC guests have distinctive public
  voices. The pre-drop thread for this archetype should quote or reference
  their most contrarian public claim and ask whether the data has validated
  it. That framing generates engagement from both believers and skeptics.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Macro Hook** — VCs aren't usually the macro hook. Don't set the episode
  up as a macro comment unless this particular guest has been visibly public
  on a macro view.

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — a thesis they've published that the market has since
    challenged, framed as a data question, not a criticism
  → Data story — a portfolio-adjacent metric (TVL, revenue, token unlock
    schedule) that's surprising relative to the category narrative

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with their most quoted/argued public claim,
    then frame the episode as the place it gets tested
    Example structure:
    Tweet 1: The contrarian claim they made [date]
    Tweet 2: What's happened in the market since
    Tweet 3: The question this episode addresses
    Tweet 4-5: Why their answer matters now

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — did the episode validate or complicate the
    thesis? Be honest. If they shifted their view on air, that's the story.
  → Quotable standalone — the most unexpectedly specific thing they said
    (VCs often say very general things; when they get specific, quote it)

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: mechanism piece on the specific protocol or category their
    thesis depends on. The VC provides the thesis; you explain the plumbing.
    Example: "How token value actually accrues at the L1 vs. application
    layer — and why the answer matters for every DeFi portfolio"
```

**What NOT to write:**
- A piece that reads like a fund pitch. If it could appear in their LP
  materials, reframe it as a critical mechanism question instead.
- A "X reasons Dragonfly / Framework is bullish on Y" format. That's
  fund marketing, not podcast content.
- A portfolio performance piece. You don't have the data, and these guests
  won't give it to you.

## Archetype 4 — Macro / Media Figure
*e.g., Anthony Scaramucci (SkyBridge Capital)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Episode Frame (pre-drop thread)** — These guests have high name
  recognition outside CT. The pre-drop thread can reach beyond crypto
  Twitter to mainstream finance audiences if framed around macro events
  rather than protocol specifics.
- **Counternarrative** — The "digital gold" narrative, Bitcoin correlation
  claims, and "institutional adoption" framing are all correctable with
  data. These guests often make these claims with conviction. The
  counternarrative post should engage with the data, not the person.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Data Story / Technical** — Macro guests are not usually the source
  of precise on-chain data. Don't build a data story post around their
  claims unless they've cited a specific number you can independently verify.
- **Long-form article** — Only if the mechanism they addressed is durable
  and correctable. Macro opinion pieces age badly.

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — the most widely repeated macro-crypto claim that's
    empirically weak. BTC-gold correlation, "inflation hedge" framing,
    "institutional capital waiting on the sidelines" language.
  → Macro hook — the live macro development (Fed decision, regulatory move,
    geopolitical event) that makes this episode timely

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the macro frame they're known for
    ("Bitcoin as digital gold / non-sovereign asset"), then introduce
    the episode as the place where that thesis gets pressure-tested against
    current data. Works cross-audience.

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — what did they say that surprised you?
    (These guests often have better mechanism fluency than CT assumes)
  → Quotable standalone — the single most re-tweetable line they delivered

EVERGREEN:
  → Article only if they said something mechanistically specific that deserves
    a full technical treatment. Otherwise, the macro content has a short
    shelf life.
```

**What NOT to write:**
- A price prediction piece. These guests make them; we don't amplify them.
- A celebrity-framing post ("The Mooch is back" type). These don't have
  legs with a crypto-native audience and you'll lose credibility with the
  audience you actually want.
- A piece that requires him to be right. Write pieces that are true
  regardless of whether his thesis proves out.

## Archetype 5 — DeFi Builder
*e.g., Hayden Adams (Uniswap), Stani Kulechov (Aave), Robert Leshner (Compound)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — DeFi builders operate in the most narrative-dense
  part of crypto. TVL inflation, emissions-as-yield, governance theater,
  and fee switch hype are all correctable with mechanism. The counternarrative
  post for this archetype should always have a number in it — the specific
  TVL figure ex-incentives, the actual revenue split, the real yield
  percentage. These land hardest when the guest is well-known enough that
  their claims are being widely repeated.
- **Long-Form Article** — DeFi mechanisms are complex enough that a thread
  often can't do the topic justice without oversimplifying. These episodes
  regularly generate one article-worthy mechanism explanation per guest.
  The best angle is usually: "here's how [the thing they built] actually
  works, and here's what the narrative gets wrong about it."

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Macro Hook** — DeFi builders rarely speak to macro conditions as their
  primary frame. Don't set a macro context post up unless the episode
  genuinely has a macro hook (e.g., rising rates affecting on-chain yield).

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — correct a specific TVL, yield, or governance claim
    circulating about their protocol or category. Use the real number.
  → Data story — protocol revenue, real yield figure, or fee switch impact
    stated plainly against what CT narrative says

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the mechanism tension that makes this
    episode necessary. Not "we're talking to the founder of X" — lead with
    the unresolved question their protocol forces (governance vs. shipping,
    LPs vs. searchers, real yield vs. emissions)

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — focus on the governance or value accrual question.
    Did they say anything precise about where the revenue actually goes?
    That's the story.
  → Quotable standalone — the most technically specific claim they made about
    their own protocol's mechanics (not the vision, the mechanism)

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: the mechanism piece — "how [AMM / lending protocol / governance
    model] actually works and what the standard explanation gets wrong"
    This is the highest-trust content type for a crypto-native audience.
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A protocol feature announcement piece. That's their blog post, not yours.
- A "why [Protocol] will win DeFi" take. You don't know that and neither
  do they. Write about the mechanism, not the outcome.
- A governance recap without a mechanism angle. "The DAO voted to do X" is
  not a story. "Here's what the voting structure means for who actually
  controls X" is.

## Archetype 6 — Exchange Executive
*e.g., Brian Armstrong (Coinbase), Richard Teng (Binance), Jesse Powell (Kraken)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — Exchange claims are among the most aggressively
  marketed in crypto. Volume figures, "regulated" language, proof-of-reserves
  framing, and "institutional adoption" stats all deserve a counternarrative
  post with the mechanism-level correction. These perform well because they
  address claims the audience sees constantly.
- **Data Story** — Coinbase's custody concentration (~70% of BTC ETF AUM),
  exchange self-reported volume vs. adjusted volume, stablecoin reserve
  yield revenue, institutional vs. retail revenue split — all of these are
  data stories that land against the marketing narrative.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Long-Form Article** — Exchange mechanics are well-documented. The article
  angle only works if the episode surfaced something genuinely new about
  how the plumbing works — a specific custody architecture detail, a
  regulatory disclosure, a new product mechanic. Otherwise skip it.

**Calendar shape for this archetype:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — one exchange claim that's technically incomplete.
    Best candidates: PoR without liabilities, "regulated" without
    specifying jurisdiction and entity, volume figures without wash-trade
    adjustment methodology
  → Data story — the concentration / revenue / custody number that
    surprises. State it plainly, one implication.

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the structural question the exchange
    sits at: regulatory pressure, custody concentration, DEX competition,
    or the specific market structure conflict the guest has been publicly
    navigating. Not "we're talking to the CEO of X" — lead with the
    unresolved tension.
    
POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — did they say anything precise about the
    conflict-of-interest question (venue + market maker) or the systemic
    risk question (custody concentration)? That's the post. If they didn't,
    the thread is about what they wouldn't answer.
  → Quotable standalone — the most legally precise thing they said, or
    the most candid admission about operational constraints

EVERGREEN:
  → Article only if they revealed something mechanistically new: a custody
    architecture detail, a regulatory path that hasn't been reported, or
    a product structure that changes how market participants should think
    about execution on their venue.
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A regulatory reaction piece ("Exchange X responds to SEC action"). This
  ages in 24 hours and you're not a news outlet.
- A "how Exchange X plans to win" take. That's their PR. Write about the
  structural dynamics their position creates, not their stated strategy.
- A founder biography framing. Armstrong, Teng, and Powell are not
  interesting because of their origin stories. They're interesting because
  of the structural positions they occupy.

## Archetype 7 — L1 / Infrastructure Founder
*e.g., Anatoly Yakovenko (Solana), Emin Gün Sirer (Avalanche)*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — L1 founders are the most active generators of
  performance claims that don't survive mechanism scrutiny. TPS figures
  that include vote transactions, "decentralization" framing that ignores
  superminority threshold data, and client diversity claims that don't
  reflect mainnet reality. These posts land hardest because they address
  claims the founder themselves has made publicly.
- **Long-Form Article** — Base-layer architecture tradeoffs (validator
  hardware requirements, consensus design, slashing policy) are too
  complex for a thread and don't age quickly. These episodes generate
  the best durable mechanism articles.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Macro Hook** — L1 founders rarely connect to macro directly. The
  "why now" for the episode is almost always a protocol milestone
  (upgrade announcement, client release, governance decision), not a
  macro event.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — the specific performance or decentralization claim
    that doesn't survive the mechanism check. Use the actual data.
  → Data story — superminority threshold count, validator hardware cost,
    actual sustained TPS under real load. State the number plainly.

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the upgrade or architectural decision
    that makes this episode necessary. Frame it as an unresolved design
    tension, not a product announcement.

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — the specific tradeoff they admitted to.
    L1 founders almost always have one moment of candor about what
    their architecture can't do. That's the thread.
  → Quotable standalone — the most mechanistically precise claim they made

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: "What [L1]'s architecture actually trades away — and why
    that tradeoff was the right call (or wasn't)"
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A TPS comparison piece. These are the most widely mocked content type
  in technical crypto circles. Write about mechanism, not the benchmark.
- A "why [L1] will win" take. Nobody knows. Write about the design choices.

## Archetype 8 — Regulator / Policy / Legal
*e.g., Hester Peirce (SEC), Jake Chervinsky (Variant), Brian Brooks*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — Policy language is full of terms that mean different
  things to different audiences. "Regulated exchange," "proof of reserves,"
  "regulatory clarity," and "trust charter" are all correctable with the
  specific legal definition. These posts reach the widest audience because
  the misconceptions are most widespread.
- **Long-Form Article** — Regulatory mechanism pieces have long shelf lives.
  "What the GENIUS Act actually requires" or "The difference between a trust
  charter and a full bank license" are reference pieces people link to for
  months.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Data Story** — Policy guests don't usually generate data-driven content
  unless they've cited specific enforcement stats or rulemaking timelines.
  Don't force it.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — the specific regulatory framing CT has wrong.
    Use the actual statutory text or agency guidance as the correction.
  → Macro hook — the live regulatory development (bill passage, enforcement
    action, court ruling) that makes this episode timely

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the specific legal tension the guest
    sits at (industry advocate vs. regulator, former agency staff vs.
    current role). The "why them, why now" for policy guests is almost
    always a live legislative or enforcement moment.

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — what did they say that they don't normally
    say on record? Policy guests occasionally drop specific case
    references or internal process details that aren't in public comments.
  → Quotable standalone — their most precise legal claim, quoted exactly

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: the mechanism piece. "What [regulation/act/ruling] actually
    means for [protocol/exchange/stablecoin issuer]"
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A reaction piece to an enforcement action. That's the 24-hour news
  cycle. Write about the mechanism the enforcement action reveals.
- A "crypto needs better regulation" or "crypto is over-regulated" take.
  Both are opinion, neither is content.

## Archetype 9 — Bitcoin Native / OG
*e.g., Michael Saylor (Strategy), Nic Carter (Castle Island), Saifedean Ammous*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — The Bitcoin-native audience is large and highly
  engaged, and the counternarrative that performs best here is the internal
  critique: claims Bitcoin OGs make about their own ecosystem that don't
  survive mechanism scrutiny. Lightning adoption numbers vs. narrative,
  the security budget post-subsidy math, the "digital gold" correlation
  claim on 6-month windows. These outperform external critiques because
  they come from inside the thesis.
- **Data Story** — For Saylor specifically: Strategy's BTC-per-share metric,
  the convertible note structure, the premium/NAV ratio. These are tracked
  publicly and the numbers are surprising to most CT audiences.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Long-Form Article** — Bitcoin monetary theory pieces age slowly but
  are extremely competitive. Only write the long-form piece if the episode
  surfaced a mechanistically new claim (e.g., a specific security budget
  number, a Lightning routing success rate) — not a philosophical argument
  that's been made in a hundred other pieces.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — the Bitcoin claim that's empirically testable and
    currently being made without the test. Security budget math, Lightning
    routing data, BTC-Nasdaq correlation during the last drawdown.
  → Data story — for Saylor: BTC per share, note structure, premium to NAV.
    For Nic Carter: on-chain data point that contradicts a narrative claim.

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the mechanism tension, not the
    personality. "Can Bitcoin's security model survive the subsidy
    disappearing?" performs better than "We're talking to Michael Saylor."

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — the one moment they engaged with mechanism
    rather than narrative. Bitcoin OG interviews almost always have one.
  → Quotable standalone — their single sharpest claim about Bitcoin's
    properties, stated without the surrounding sermon

EVERGREEN:
  → Article only if a mechanistically new claim was made. Otherwise skip.
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A price target piece. Full stop.
- A "Bitcoin maximalism: right or wrong?" take. This is not a debate club.
  Write about the mechanism.
- A Saylor biography or "how Strategy built its Bitcoin position" narrative.
  The interesting content is the capital structure, not the story.

## Archetype 10 — Prediction Markets Developer
*e.g., Shayne Coplan (Polymarket), Kalshi team, UMA Protocol*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Data Story** — Prediction markets generate the most quotable data in
  all of crypto: election probabilities, OI at specific moments, accuracy
  comparisons to polls. These data stories reach far beyond CT because
  mainstream audiences understand political and economic event markets.
  State the number and one implication.
- **Counternarrative** — "Prediction markets beat polls" is the dominant
  CT narrative. It's correct on average but not uniformly true, and the
  specific accuracy metrics are rarely cited. The counternarrative post
  should give the number, the methodology, and the sample — not the
  general claim.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Long-Form Article** — Prediction market mechanism pieces (LMSR, oracle
  design) are technically dense and have a small audience. Only write it
  if the episode surfaced something new about oracle architecture or
  manipulation surface.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Data story — a live prediction market with meaningful OI and a number
    that surprises. State the probability and what it implies.
  → Counternarrative — the "prediction markets beat polls" claim with the
    actual accuracy metric and sample behind it

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with a live market or a recently resolved
    one with high OI. The "why now" for this archetype is almost always
    a live event market or a regulatory development (Kalshi CFTC ruling).

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — the oracle or manipulation question. Did they
    say anything specific about how they handle contested resolutions?
    That's the thread.
  → Quotable standalone — their most precise claim about market accuracy
    or manipulation resistance

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: oracle design and the manipulation surface. This is the
    highest-value mechanism piece in this space and the most under-covered.
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A "prediction markets will change democracy" take. That's an op-ed for
  a general publication, not crypto-native content.
- A recap of a resolved prediction market result. News, not analysis.

## Archetype 11 — Privacy Developer / Advocate
*e.g., Zooko Wilcox (Zcash), Roman Storm (Tornado Cash), Aztec team*

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Counternarrative** — Privacy in crypto is the most mischaracterized
  topic in regulation and media. The conflation of privacy with anonymity,
  the "trustless privacy" claim for SNARK systems with trusted setups, and
  the "Tornado Cash is censorship" framing vs. "Tornado Cash processed
  sanctioned funds" factual record — all are correctable and all perform
  well because the audience has strong priors in both directions.
- **Long-Form Article** — The legal and technical distinction between
  privacy-preserving technology and sanctions evasion is genuinely complex
  and demands more than a thread. "What the Tornado Cash ruling actually
  means for ZK privacy protocols" is a reference piece with a long shelf life.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Data Story** — Privacy protocols don't generate clean public data by
  design. Don't force a data-driven post if the numbers aren't there.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Counternarrative — the specific privacy claim that doesn't survive the
    legal or technical check. Trusted setup assumptions, the Zcash
    transparent address adoption data, or the Tornado Cash legal theory.
  → Macro hook — the live regulatory or legal development in privacy (court
    ruling, OFAC action, MiCA privacy token guidance)

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the legal tension, not the technology.
    "The government sanctioned a smart contract. Here's what that means
    for every privacy protocol" reaches far beyond the privacy-specific
    audience.

POST-DROP:
  → Post-interview thread — the compliance-privacy design question. Did
    they say anything specific about what "regulatory auditability" actually
    means in their architecture? That's the most interesting moment in
    these interviews.
  → Quotable standalone — their most precise statement about the legal
    distinction between their protocol and Tornado Cash

EVERGREEN:
  → Article: the mechanism + legal piece. "What ZK privacy actually proves
    and what it doesn't — and why the legal theory matters for every
    protocol with private state"
```

**What NOT to Write:**
- A "privacy is a human right" op-ed. True, but not the content your
  crypto-native audience needs.
- A "here's why Tornado Cash did nothing wrong" take without the legal
  mechanism. Opinion without mechanism ages badly.

## Archetype 12 — Hyperliquid Builder
*e.g., Hyperliquid core team, TradeXYZ/Unit, Felix Protocol, Ventuals, Trove*

**Context to load before planning:**
HIP-3 launched October 2025. Within three months: >$1B OI, ~$25B total
volume, >$3M fees. Single-day record $5.2B (Feb 5, 2026) driven by
precious metals trading as gold broke $5,000/oz and silver broke $100.
This is now a traditional asset distribution story as much as a DeFi one.
The active HIP-3 front ends — TradeXYZ (Unit), Felix Protocol, Ventuals —
each made different oracle and collateral choices. HyperEVM launched mainnet
March 2026. HYPE team unlocks running ~$200M/month since November 2025.

**Strongest seeds:**
- **Data Story** — HIP-3 produces some of the cleanest, most dramatic
  on-chain data in crypto: single-day volume records, OI concentration
  by market, metals trading volume during macro stress events, HYPE
  buyback/burn rate vs. team unlock supply. These numbers are all
  transparent and on-chain, contrasting sharply with CEX opacity.
  The February 5 volume spike during the metals move is the most
  compelling recent example — state the number, the asset class, and
  one implication about what it means for on-chain traditional finance.
- **Counternarrative** — "24/7 equity trading" and "decentralized stocks"
  framing is everywhere. The mechanism-level correction — HIP-3 equity
  markets are perps referencing a price feed, not actual stock ownership,
  with oracle-dependent settlement and no corporate action protections —
  is the most important counternarrative in this space right now.

**Weakest seeds:**
- **Macro Hook** — The macro IS the story for HIP-3 metals and equities,
  but it should be framed as a product-market-fit observation, not a
  separate macro post. Don't produce a standalone macro hook post; fold
  the macro context into the data story or pre-drop thread directly.

**Calendar shape:**
```
PRE-RECORDING:
  → Data story — the HIP-3 volume/OI number that surprises. Best versions:
    (a) silver single-day volume vs. total Hyperliquid crypto perp volume
        on the same day — shows how fast traditional assets moved the needle
    (b) XYZ100 OI vs. legacy crypto market OI as a ratio — shows where
        the new growth is coming from
    (c) HYPE net supply change: buyback/burn from HIP-3 fees vs. monthly
        team unlock — the actual tokenomics picture
  → Counternarrative — "HIP-3 equity markets are not stocks. Here's what
    you actually own, what happens during a trading halt, and why the
    oracle architecture is the risk you're not thinking about."

PRE-DROP:
  → Pre-interview thread — lead with the market event that makes this
    episode timely. For HIP-3 builders: the metals spike, the equity
    market launches, or the first major oracle stress event. Frame it
    as: "here's the moment traditional finance arrived on-chain, here's
    who built the rails, here's what the rails actually are."
    
    Suggested structure:
    Tweet 1: The data point — gold at $X, silver at $Y, $Zbn on-chain
             in 24 hours on a DEX most people haven't heard of
```

## Cross-Archetype Calendar Rules

**Minimum content plan requirements:**
- At least 1 counternarrative piece in every calendar
- At least 1 data story in every calendar
- No two pieces making the same argument in different formats
- "What NOT to Write" section must exist in every plan

**The pre-drop thread is always required.**
Structure for every archetype:
```
Tweet 1: The live context — why this matters right now (macro event, data point,
         product launch, regulatory development)
Tweet 2: The specific tension this guest sits at
Tweet 3: The question the episode actually answers
Tweet 4-5: Why their perspective specifically — what they know that others don't
Tweet 6: The listen/watch CTA
```

**Timing triggers:**
- Counternarrative + data posts: 1-2 weeks before recording (while the topic
  is live, not cold)
- Pre-drop thread: 48-72 hours before the episode publishes
- Post-drop thread + quotable: within 24-48 hours of drop
- Evergreen article: 1-2 weeks after drop, or anytime it's linkable
