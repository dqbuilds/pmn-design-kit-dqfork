# Question Craft — Non-Leading Interview Technique

Load this file whenever generating interview questions for any guest.
These principles govern how questions are constructed, sequenced, and delivered.
They apply to all five question types in `workflows/guest-diligence.md`.

---

## The Simplicity Principle — The Guest Is the Show

The listener didn't tune in for you. They tuned in for the guest.

That one fact governs everything about how questions should be constructed.
The question is not the content. The question is the door. Your only job is
to open it as wide as possible and step out of the way.

A complex question competes with the guest. A simple question serves them.

**The test:** If you removed the question and just read the answer aloud,
would the listener understand the shape of what was asked? If yes, the
question is simple enough. If no, you've buried the subject under your
own framing.

**The discipline:** For every question drafted, ask: what is the simplest
possible way to ask this? Not the cleverest. Not the most impressive.
The simplest. Then ask that.

A guest who is a genuine subject matter expert will take a simple, open
question somewhere you couldn't have predicted. A complex question narrows
the range of answers before the guest has a chance to go where they
actually wanted to go.

Long questions shift the focus to the host. The guest spends the first
part of their answer orienting to the question's complexity instead of
answering. The listener, meanwhile, has already started losing the thread.

Simple questions sound like they come from someone who genuinely wants
to know the answer — which makes the guest feel worth listening to.
Complex questions sound like performance. And once the listener senses
performance, the episode stops being a conversation and starts being
a demonstration.

**The compression test:** Take any draft question. Try to cut it in half.
If the meaning survives, the original was too long. Ask the shorter version.

## Psychological Dynamics — What to Manage in Yourself and in the Guest

These are not tricks. They are patterns to recognize and manage — both in how
you behave as an interviewer and in how the guest responds to you.

---

### What's Happening in You

**The performance trap**
The most common failure mode. Asking to seem smart rather than to learn.
The question becomes a vehicle for displaying research. The guest gets less;
you look more prepared. Fix: your research should be invisible in the question.

**Anxiety-filling**
Silence after a short answer feels like failure. The instinct is to rescue
the moment with a follow-up or reframe. Don't. The guest who gives a thin
first answer is often testing the water. The silence is pressure. Let it work.
The real answer usually comes in the second 30 seconds.

**Confirmation bias in real time**
You built a thesis in prep. When the guest confirms it, you feel validated and
move on. When they contradict it, you unconsciously smooth it over. The
friction is where the episode lives. Train yourself to notice contradictions
and lean into them rather than past them.

**Recency loading**
Whatever the guest said last is what you respond to. A strong interviewer holds
the whole conversation in mind and returns to things said earlier that didn't
get resolved. "You said X twenty minutes ago — how does that square with what
you just said?" Most interviewers never make this move.

---

### What's Happening in the Guest

**The press release default**
Every guest has a prepared version of themselves. It comes out in response to
expected questions. Your prep exists specifically to recognize when the press
release answer has been deployed — so you know to probe rather than accept.

**Social mirroring**
Guests calibrate to the energy and framing you set. Warm questions open them.
Suspicious questions close them. If you reward safe answers with enthusiasm,
you train the guest to stay in safe territory. Stay neutral longer than feels
polite. Don't signal what answer you want.

**Authority accommodation**
Smart, confident guests will still soften their actual view to match what they
perceive as yours. If you signal agreement early, they'll assume you want
validation and stop pushing their real position.

**The humble brag setup**
Guests often set up accomplishments with fake modesty — "I wasn't sure this
would work, but..." This is a framing device to make the success land harder.
Don't treat the self-deprecation as real information.

**Exhaustion-induced honesty**
Long interviews get more candid toward the end. Energy management drops and
the maintained narrative relaxes. Save the question you most need an honest
answer to for later in the conversation — not the formal adversarial slot, but
naturally, when the guest has lowered their guard through familiarity.

---

### Structural Dynamics to Watch

**The false completion**
The guest finishes a sentence but hasn't actually answered the question. The
answer was a reframe, or a related point, or an answer to a slightly different
question. The instinct is to accept and move on. Don't. Let the silence sit,
or return directly: "That's the mechanism — but what I was asking was the
decision. How did you make that call?"

**Topic anchoring**
The first few questions set the mental frame for the rest of the interview —
for you and for the guest. If you open on brand, you're doing a brand
interview. If you open on mechanism, you're doing a mechanism interview.
Opening questions aren't warm-up. They set the register of the whole
conversation. Choose deliberately.

**The concession that costs you**
"So what I'm hearing is X" gives the guest the chance to revise what they
said into a safer version. You meant to confirm; you've let them edit.
Trust what you heard. Ask the follow-up without recapping first.

**Flattery debt**
Complimenting a guest early creates obligation — they'll give you a softer
interview in return. Warmth and flattery are different. Be warm. Don't
create debt.

---

### The One Rule Under All of Them

The guest is always managing how they're perceived. Your job is to make honest
self-representation feel safer than managed self-presentation.

The question that makes honesty easier than deflection — that's the goal.
Not exposure. Not gotcha. Not performance.

When the guest feels genuinely curious about — not evaluated by — the person
asking, they say things they haven't said before. That's the interview worth
recording.

## Active Tense — Always

Questions must be written in active tense. Passive constructions distance the guest
from their own decisions and make it easier to give a vague, institutional answer.
Active tense puts the guest in the sentence — as the subject, the decision-maker,
the person who acted.

**The rule:** If the guest isn't the subject of the question, rewrite it.

### Passive vs. Active — Examples

> ❌ Passive: "How has tokenization been approached by institutions?"
> ✅ Active: "How are institutions actually using tokenization right now?"

> ❌ Passive: "What challenges have been encountered in building the regulated stack?"
> ✅ Active: "What broke first when you tried to build the regulated stack?"

> ❌ Passive: "What was the EU approval process like?"
> ✅ Active: "Walk me through how you got three regulators — CNMV, ESMA, and the ECB — to approve this simultaneously."

> ❌ Passive: "What decisions were made around chain selection?"
> ✅ Active: "Why did you pick Avalanche for the EU platform over every other chain on your list?"

Active tense also applies to follow-ups. Never say "It was mentioned that..." — say
"You said X — what did you mean by that?"

## The Core Problem with Leading Questions

A leading question gives the guest something to react to instead of something
to reveal. When you load your thesis into the question, you've already filled
the space where candor would have lived. The guest will agree, qualify, or
push back — but none of those are the same as them telling you what they
actually think unprompted.

The anxiety that drives leading questions is the anxiety to seem smart in
front of a smart guest. The question becomes a performance of your own
knowledge instead of a tool for extracting theirs.

**The rule:** the more you reveal about what you already think, the less
you'll learn about what they actually know.

---

## The Three Leading Question Patterns to Avoid

### Pattern 1 — Thesis-First
> "Given that institutional adoption is clearly accelerating, how do you
> see your protocol benefiting from that?"

You've handed them a narrative and asked them to inhabit it. A guest who
disagrees will soften their disagreement out of politeness. A guest who
agrees just validates what you already believe. Either way: almost nothing learned.

**Fix:** Remove the thesis. Ask about the mechanism.
> "What does your user acquisition actually look like right now?"

---

### Pattern 2 — Compliment-Loaded
> "You've built something really impressive here — what drove that decision?"

The flattery primes them to tell you a success story. What you've killed is
the chance they'd lead with a failure, a regret, or a complication — which
is almost always more interesting.

**Fix:** Ask about the decision without framing its outcome.
> "Walk me through how that decision got made."

---

### Pattern 3 — Compound Question
> "How do you think about the competitive landscape, and do you think L2s
> are actually commoditized at this point, and what does that mean for your roadmap?"

They'll answer whichever sub-question they're most comfortable with, usually
the last one. You never find out what they actually think about the first.

**Fix:** One question at a time. Ask the hardest one first.

---

## The Play-Dumb Posture

Arrive deeply informed — so you can recognize when an answer is incomplete,
inconsistent, or surprising. But that research should not appear in the question.

Santiago's move with Do Kwon: he knew the white paper, had invested in Haven,
had real familiarity. Still walked in asking basic questions — not because he
didn't know, but because he wanted to see how the person handled being asked
to explain themselves from the ground up.

The gap between what a founder claims and what they can actually explain
from first principles is where the real information lives.

The play-dumb posture also puts the burden of clarity on the guest. If they
can't explain their mechanism to someone pretending not to know, they probably
don't understand it well enough — or they're hiding something.

**Applied to question writing:** frame questions as if you have no prior
thesis. The question should create a vacuum the guest has to fill.

## Five Concrete Techniques

### 1. Open with a Scenario, Not a Thesis
> ❌ "Given how crowded the perp DEX space has gotten, how do you differentiate?"
> ✅ "Walk me through how you think about competition."

The first frames the conclusion you want them to confirm.
The second makes them tell you what they actually see.

---

### 2. Ask About the Mechanism, Not the Narrative
> ❌ "Has institutional adoption been a tailwind for you?"
> ✅ "What does your user acquisition actually look like right now?"

Narratives are easy to spin. Mechanisms are harder. Specificity is the enemy
of the rehearsed answer.

---

### 3. Sit in Silence After a Short Answer
A guest who gives a thin first response is often testing the water. If you
jump in with the next question, you've relieved the pressure. If you let the
silence sit for three seconds, you'll often get the real answer.

Applied in question writing: don't stack follow-ups into the question itself.
Let the single question breathe. Trust the silence to do the work.

---

### 4. Ask What They'd Need to Believe for the Opposite to Be True
> "What would have to be true for your thesis to be wrong?"
> "What's the bear case for what you're building?"

Most guests have never been asked this directly. The ones who answer well
have the most durable conviction. The ones who deflect tell you something
important too. This is Santi's asymmetry check applied to conversation —
draw out the downside before accepting the upside framing.

---

### 5. Don't Recap Before the Follow-Up
When you say "So if I'm understanding correctly, you're saying X..." you've
given the guest a chance to slightly reframe, sand down the edges, and agree
with a softened version of what they said.

Just ask the follow-up. Trust that you heard them correctly. The recap is
hospitality that costs you information.

## The Edutainment Standard — Three People in the Room

Every question must work simultaneously for three people:

1. **The guest** — specific enough to require a real answer, not a rehearsed one
2. **The host** — advances the conversation and signals genuine curiosity
3. **The listener** — who may be crypto-curious but not deep in the news cycle,
   who came to learn something and to be engaged, not just to overhear an expert
   interview

Questions that work only for the first two fail the show.

The register of a great podcast question is curious and energetic, not
prosecutorial. Hard questions can be warm. Skepticism can be delivered with
genuine interest. The listener should feel like they're in a room with
fascinating people having a real conversation — not watching an interrogation.

### The framing sentence

A single sentence of listener-facing context before the question is allowed —
encouraged — when the subject requires it. It is not a thesis. It is a
signpost that tells the listener why the next 90 seconds will matter to them.

> ❌ No context, cold open: "What does your derivatives volume tell you about
>    product-market fit?"
> ✅ Listener framing + question: "Most people picture a Binance user buying
>    Bitcoin. But two-thirds of the volume is perpetual futures — leveraged bets
>    on price with no expiry date. Walk me through who that person actually is."

The framing sentence teaches the listener what a perpetual future is, flags the
gap between perception and reality, and sets up the question — all before the
guest opens their mouth. The question itself is still single and specific.

### Emotional register — what to aim for

Before finalizing any question, ask:
- Does this have a feeling attached to it — curiosity, stakes, surprise,
  wonder, or tension?
- Is there a concrete image or scenario in the question, or is it purely abstract?
- Would a listener who hasn't followed this story want to hear the answer?
- Could the listener repeat the question to a friend later — "they asked him
  about X and what he said was..."?

Upbeat does not mean soft. A question about a difficult topic can still be
delivered with energy, genuine curiosity, and warmth. The goal is questions
the listener feels lucky to be hearing, not questions that make them feel
like they're watching a congressional hearing.

## The Einstein Principle

Spend 80-90% of your preparation time formulating the right question.
The answer will come much faster than that last 10%.

A well-constructed question does the work before the guest opens their mouth:
- Open enough that you can't predict the answer
- Specific enough that a vague non-answer is obviously a non-answer
- Creates a situation where honesty is easier than deflection

The question that looks naive is often the most sophisticated one. The question
that reveals your own knowledge is almost always the weakest one.

## Question Scoring — Harmonic Mean Framework

Every question has two dimensions. Both must be high. Sacrificing one for the other
produces either an interrogation (high signal, low humanity) or a therapy session
(high humanity, low signal). Neither makes great audio.

**Signal score (1–10):** Does this require a non-generic answer? Does it force the
guest to name a specific mechanism, metric, or decision? Can it be answered with a
rehearsed talking point?

**Human score (1–10):** Does this lower the guest's guard? Does it feel like a real
conversation? Does it have emotional texture — curiosity, stakes, wonder, tension,
or delight? Would a listener who hasn't followed this story lean in to hear the
answer? Would a non-specialist understand why it matters before the guest finishes
their first sentence? A question that is technically accessible but emotionally flat
scores no higher than 7 on this dimension.

**Scoring method: Harmonic Mean**
Formula: H = 2(S × H) / (S + H)

Use harmonic mean, not arithmetic. A 9 signal / 5 human scores **6.4**, not 7.0.
Imbalance is penalized because imbalanced questions fail on one dimension.

**Minimum target: 8.5 on both signal AND human score.**
At 8.5/8.5 the harmonic mean is 8.5. At 9/9 it is 9.0. That is the range to aim for.

### How to reach 8.5+ on both dimensions simultaneously

**When signal is high but humanity is low:**
Find the personal angle inside the mechanism. A question about authorization rates
becomes human when framed around the first payment the guest remembers that worked.
The mechanism question and the memory question can be the same question.

**When humanity is high but signal is low:**
Add a specific constraint that forces a real answer — a number, a time, a product
decision, a named outcome. "What's been your biggest challenge?" is warm but soft.
"What's been your biggest challenge since the February launch?" forces a real answer
inside the same warm framing.

### Two-question rule

For each question slot, generate two versions:

- **Version A — humanity-led:** Opens with a personal frame (a memory, a moment,
  a decision), with the signal embedded inside it. The guest doesn't feel the
  mechanism question until they're already answering.

- **Version B — signal-led:** Opens with a specific constraint or mechanism, with
  generous framing that gives the guest permission to be honest rather than defensive.

Present both. Let the host choose. Both must score 8.5+ on both dimensions before
being presented — if either version fails the threshold, rewrite it.

### Worked example

**Topic:** A payments exec's career transition from TradFi to crypto.

> ❌ Version A (humanity 9 / signal 4 / HM 5.5):
> "What drew you to this space?"

> ❌ Version B (signal 9 / humanity 4 / HM 5.5):
> "At Visa you optimized authorization rates — what's the equivalent metric on your
> current platform and how does it compare?"

> ✅ Version A revised (humanity 9 / signal 8.5 / HM 8.7):
> "What's the first payment you remember thinking actually worked the way it should?"

> ✅ Version B revised (signal 9.5 / humanity 8.5 / HM 9.0):
> "The platform's been live for six weeks. Are people using it the way you designed
> it — or differently?"

The first revised version hides its signal inside a personal memory.
The second hides its technical demand inside a generous framing.
Both score above 8.5 on both dimensions.

## Question Quality Check

Before finalizing any question, run it through this test:

- [ ] Is the question in active tense? (If no — rewrite so the guest is the subject)
- [ ] Does this question contain my thesis? (If yes — remove the thesis)
- [ ] Does this question contain a compliment or positive framing? (If yes — strip it)
- [ ] Is this more than one question? (If yes — split it, ask the hardest one. Compound questions let the guest pick which sub-question to answer — they will always pick the easier one. Use "and" in a question only when the two parts are genuinely inseparable.)
- [ ] Could the guest answer this with a rehearsed talking point? (If yes — get more specific)
- [ ] Does the question reveal what I already think? (If yes — rewrite as a scenario or mechanism)
- [ ] Does the question carry neutral-to-positive connotation? (If accusatory — reframe)
- [ ] If this question touches on allegations, legal proceedings, or public controversy — does it name the allegation directly? (If yes — rewrite as a mechanism or decision question. See Controversy Question Protocol below.)
- [ ] What would a non-answer look like? (If non-answer is hard to detect — rewrite)
- [ ] Signal score: X/10 — Human score: X/10 — Harmonic mean: X (minimum 8.5 both)

## Psychological Framing — The Guest's Mental State Determines Answer Quality

This is the principle that governs all questions, but especially the hard ones.

A guest who feels interrogated gives polished, rehearsed answers. They protect
themselves. The interview becomes a performance. A guest who feels genuinely
curious about — not suspicious of — opens up. They say things they haven't
said before. They correct themselves mid-answer. They admit complications.
That's the interview worth recording.

**The rule:** the question's job is not to expose the guest. It's to make
honesty easier than deflection. Those are different goals and they require
different framing.

---

### Neutral-to-Positive Connotation — Always

Every question, including the hardest ones, should carry a neutral-to-positive
connotation. This does not mean softening the question or removing its teeth.
It means removing the threat signal so the guest doesn't spend their answer
defending instead of revealing.

**Expressing concern or doubt is allowed — accusatory framing is not.**

There is a meaningful difference between a question that voices a legitimate
concern and one that puts the guest on trial. Concern comes from genuine
curiosity about something unresolved. Accusation comes from a verdict already
reached. The first opens the guest up. The second closes them down.

> ❌ Accusatory: "Critics say your TVL numbers are misleading — how do you respond?"
> ✅ Concern-led: "Where do you think your TVL overstates real protocol health?"

> ❌ Accusatory: "Your SPAC valuation looks aggressive given your revenue multiple."
> ✅ Concern-led: "What's the bear case on the $1.25B valuation — what would have to go wrong for that to look expensive in 18 months?"

The concern version assumes the guest has already wrestled with the hard version
of the question privately. It invites the honest answer they've already formed.
The accusatory version assumes guilt and asks for a defense.

**The psychological mechanism:**
- A question that sounds like an accusation triggers defense
- A question that sounds like genuine curiosity triggers reflection
- A question that voices concern without assigning blame invites the guest
  to locate the problem themselves — which always produces a better answer

The hard question and the warm question are not opposites. The best hard
questions feel warm while containing something sharp underneath.

**Three framing moves that change psychological temperature without changing
the substance of the question:**

### Move 1 — Assume Competence, Not Failure

> ❌ "Critics say your network has a centralization problem."
> ✅ "You have 350 validators. What does the decentralization picture actually look like from the inside?"

The first puts the guest on trial. The second assumes they've looked at
their own data honestly and invites them to share what they see. The
information you're after is identical. The answer quality is not.

### Move 2 — Give Permission to Admit Complexity

> ❌ "Is Telegram's involvement in TON governance a conflict of interest?"
> ✅ "What's the most complicated part of managing the Telegram relationship from a governance standpoint?"

The first requires the guest to either agree (damaging) or deny (defensive).
The second assumes complexity exists — which it does — and invites them to
locate it themselves. They'll often go further than the original question
would have reached.

### Move 3 — Frame Difficulty as Experience, Not Failure

> ❌ "Your transaction volume dropped 53% from the December peak. What went wrong?"
> ✅ "The volume peak in December was driven by tap-to-earn. What does the baseline look like now that that cycle has wound down?"

The first asks them to account for a failure. The second contextualizes the
decline as a natural cycle and asks what's underneath it. The guest who
answers the second version will tell you everything the first version was
trying to extract — without the defensive crouch.

### Controversy Question Protocol

When a question touches on allegations, legal proceedings, regulatory action,
press reports, or public controversy, the neutral-to-positive rule is hardest
to apply and most important to enforce.

**The failure mode: naming the allegation in the question.**

Doing so immediately activates the guest's defense posture. They have a legal
team. They have a prepared rebuttal. You will get the rebuttal — not the truth.

**The fix: ask about the mechanism or the decision, not the allegation.**

Instead of naming what was reported, ask about the underlying tension, the
structural challenge, or the judgment call. A guest who is being honest about
a hard situation will often surface the substance themselves — in their own
words, which is always more valuable than a prepared denial.

> ❌ Allegation-forward: "Reports say your firm fired compliance staff who
> flagged sanctioned transactions — how do you respond?"
> ✅ Mechanism-led: "What's the hardest call you've had to make in the
> compliance function since taking over — where the right answer wasn't obvious?"

> ❌ Allegation-forward: "The DOJ settlement suggests the old Binance had serious
> control failures. What actually changed?"
> ✅ Mechanism-led: "When you inherited the compliance program, what was the
> first thing you changed — and what took the longest to actually fix?"

The mechanism question and the allegation question are after the same
information. The mechanism question will usually get further.

**Human reality check:** Before finalizing any question that touches on
controversy, ask — if I were this guest and this question arrived in a
pre-interview briefing, would I feel accused or curious? If accused: rewrite.
Curiosity opens people. Accusation closes them. The goal is always to make
honesty easier than deflection — that is true even when, especially when,
the subject is something the guest would rather not address.

---

### Applied to Every Question Type

**Warm-up:** Assume the journey was complicated, not triumphant.
> ❌ "What drew you to this exciting space?"
> ✅ "What were you working on when you decided this was the right move?"

**Core thesis:** Assume their belief is genuinely held and hasn't been fully
tested yet. Give them room to articulate it without pre-loading your verdict.
> ❌ "You believe stablecoins will replace SWIFT — is that realistic?"
> ✅ "What does the stablecoin payment corridor look like in five years if everything goes right?"

**Technical depth:** Assume they understand the mechanism and want to explain
it well. Make them the expert, not the defendant.
> ❌ "Can you actually process payments at Visa scale on-chain?"
> ✅ "Walk me through what happens to throughput when you have 10,000 simultaneous transactions."

**Contested territory:** Assume they've already wrestled with the hard version
of this question privately. Invite the honest answer they've already formed.
> ❌ "Critics say your TVL is inflated — how do you respond?"
> ✅ "Where does your current TVL overstate real protocol health?"

**Adversarial:** Assume they want to answer this honestly and give them the
framing that makes that easier rather than harder.
> ❌ "What keeps you up at night?"
> ✅ "What's the version of this that doesn't work — and how close do you think you are to that line?"

## The Adversarial Question (Q5)

The fifth question should be the one the guest least wants to answer.
The instinct is to either soften it (which loses the information) or
sharpen it into a trap (which closes the guest down).

Neither. The goal is a question where honesty is genuinely easier than
deflection — not because the question is soft, but because the framing
assumes the guest has already thought about the hard version honestly.

**The tell for a well-framed adversarial question:** the guest should
feel slightly relieved to be asked it directly, because it means they can
say the honest thing they've been carrying without it sounding like an
admission of failure.

> ❌ "Critics say you've been misleading investors about your TVL."
> ✅ "Where does your current TVL overstate real protocol health?"

The first is a gotcha. The second assumes they've already made this
distinction internally — which they have — and invites them to share it.

**Psychological test before finalizing Q5:** Would a thoughtful guest
read this question and feel: (a) attacked, (b) trapped, or (c) invited
to say something honest they haven't said publicly yet?

The answer should always be (c). If it's (a) or (b), reframe.
