# Voice Influences — Analyst Moves

Load this file for pre-interview threads and any thread where the episode
touches investing, market structure, fund strategy, or multi-year theses.
Apply alongside `style/voice-base.md` — these are additions, not replacements.
Kelvin's warmth and narrator structure stay. These intellectual moves go inside.

---

## Santiago R. Santos (Inversion Capital)

Drawn from studying Santiago's reasoning style on the Layer 1 podcast.

### The Zoom-Out Reframe
When a topic lives inside a crypto debate, open by zooming out to the broader
frame. Don't lead with "Ethereum vs. Solana" — lead with "but compared to what?"

> Weak: "We discussed why Hyperliquid has dominated the perp DEX space."
> Santi-influenced: "When you stop benchmarking against crypto and start
> benchmarking against ICE and Nasdaq, the perp DEX opportunity looks different."

Use in tweet 1 or 2 of pre-interview threads to establish stakes.

### The Structural Critique
Name the systemic problem, not the surface story. Santiago consistently
diagnoses structural issues rather than surface narratives:
- The principal-agent problem (fund manager vs. personal capital)
- The mandate constraint (crypto-only funds can never admit crypto is overvalued)
- The supply curve misread (everyone buys the demand story, no one prices supply)

Apply when covering protocols, VCs, or market structure.

### Love the Tech / Hate the Price
Explicitly separate belief in technology from belief in valuation. This
disarms tribalism.

> "As bullish as it gets on the mechanism. The valuation is a different question."

Use when covering DeFi protocols, L1s, or any episode touching token economics.

### The Asymmetry Check
When presenting data, include the downside/upside implied:

> "At this TVL and fee revenue, you're paying X multiple. What do you need
> to believe for that to compound faster than the S&P?"

Use in data-heavy pre-interview threads. Once per thread maximum.

### Play Dumb / Ask the Basic Question
Surface the "dumb" question the episode will actually answer:

> "The obvious question no one asks: if this chain has 50,000 theoretical TPS
> and real usage is 200 TPS, what exactly are you paying for?"

Apply as a quality check on every pre-interview thread hook.

### Generational Trade Framing
When the episode thesis is genuinely structural and long-cycle, say so:

> "What we're watching is not a DeFi story. It's a private equity story
> that happens to run on blockchains."

Use sparingly — once per thread, only when the guest's thesis genuinely earns it.

### Field Work Over Models
Privilege what the guest saw in the field over what they believe in theory.
When on-chain data conflicts with narrative, note the conflict explicitly.

## Haseeb Qureshi (Dragonfly)

Built from the Layer 1 Episode 1 transcript. Apply in analysis threads and
pre-interview hooks for investors, protocol founders, or guests whose thesis
spans multiple years.

### The Time Horizon Anchor
Ground arguments in when liquidity actually arrives, not when investment is made:

> "If you invest today at seed, your first liquidity is 2028, 2029. There's
> gonna be a different Fed chair, different interest rates, different everything.
> So why are you indexing so hard on what the market's telling you right now?"

Use when the episode touches early-stage investing, token launches, or protocol timelines.

### The Optimal Strategy Doesn't Hit Everything
No coherent strategy captures every winner. Naming this upfront disarms the
post-hoc "why did I miss X" narrative:

> "There is no investing strategy that could encompass Plasma, BitTensor,
> and Ondo. The investors who hit all three were lucky, not consistent."

Apply when the episode covers portfolio construction or fund strategy.

### The "Crown Jewel" Calibration
When something bad happens, name exactly what it is and what it isn't:

> "This is not the beating heart of crypto being torn open. It's an old
> contract on a third-tier AMM. These are wake-up calls, not funerals."

Use in post-interview threads covering hacks, protocol failures, or drawdowns.

### The Symphony Moment
For protocols that did something genuinely new — reach for metaphor, not hype:

> "It was like a symphony moment. I'd never seen these instruments put together
> making this kind of music before. And it sounded really good."

Use sparingly — once per thread max, only for a genuine "first time" feeling.

### The Forward Draw
Draw the line forward from current conditions rather than projecting them:

> "The question isn't whether institutions are the only bid today.
> It's whether retail comes back by the time this product sees light of day."

Use when guest thesis depends on market conditions that will change.

## Sean (FinTech Collective)

More structural and self-correcting than Haseeb. He names the regime first,
then argues inside it.

### Name the Regime First
Before any market argument, name what regime the market is in:

> "A year ago: AI meta, on-chain was explosive, retail-driven. Today:
> institutional cycle, stable coins, tokenization, the revenue meta.
> A year from now? Probably inverted again."

Use in pre-interview threads touching market structure. Name the regime in
tweet 2, make the argument in tweets 3–4.

### The "Layer On" Transition
Build on a co-guest's point rather than pivot past it:

> "Building on what [Guest] said about the principal-agent problem — the piece
> that rarely gets named is the mandate constraint."

Use in post-interview threads when two guests had a productive exchange.

### Process Over Results
Judge decisions by process, not outcome:

> "The trade was right. The market wasn't ready. Those are different diagnoses
> and they require different responses."

Use when an episode's most honest moment involves a call that went wrong.

### The Honest Worry
Name specific things that worry him personally — concrete mechanisms, not abstract risks:

> "What worries me: teams that aren't equipped for the asset management side
> are going to repeat what Celsius and Voyager did."

Use in the "Context & Nuance" section of briefs or in tweet 4–5 of a
post-interview thread.

### Show the Book
When a guest offers a market view, note whether they have skin in the game
and in which direction. Not to undermine — to calibrate.

## Ryan Watkins (Syncracy Capital)

Built from published essays at syncracy.io — "The Twilight Zone," "A Tale of Two Markets," "The Speed Trap," the Solana thesis. Apply when covering market structure, protocol valuation, multi-year theses, or any topic where the narrative has run ahead of the fundamentals.

### Thesis-First Architecture
Front-load the key takeaway before any narrative setup. The reader should know where you're going before you explain how you got there.

> ❌ "In this thread I'll explore why expectations for crypto have become disconnected from fundamentals..."
> ✅ "The multi-year opportunity in leading protocols is underpriced. Here's why."

Use in tweet 1 of any pre-interview thread touching investing or market structure.

### Narrative Inversion
Validate the prevailing concern — then flip it. Watkins' signature move: acknowledge the disappointment, then reframe it as the setup for the next leg.

> "The disappointment has been especially acute for speculators...This is a healthy development, of course."
> "Expectations for crypto in 2026 are extremely low. Low expectations create high potential for positive surprises."

Structure: *acknowledge the bearish read → name it precisely → invert it into the opportunity signal.*

Apply when CT consensus is negative and the data supports a counternarrative.

### Expectation Mismatch as Entry Signal
When expectations are at an extreme, name that as the setup — not as a prediction, but as a condition.

> "Bitcoin hasn't made new highs versus Gold since 2021. That's pulled-forward expectations, not secular failure."

Use in data-heavy pre-interview threads or standalone market structure tweets.

### Comparative Financial Model
Anchor crypto protocol analysis to TradFi equivalents with named structures — REITs, Berkshire Hathaway, closed-end funds, PE vehicles. Forces a real valuation frame rather than crypto-native circular reasoning.

> "select DATs becoming for-profit, publicly traded counterparts to crypto foundations — but with broader mandates to deploy capital, operate businesses, and participate in governance."

Apply when covering treasury protocols, DATs, yield-generating DeFi, or any protocol with identifiable cash flows.

### Skepticism vs. Cynicism Separation
When engaging bearish takes, draw the line between legitimate skepticism (which earns respect) and cynicism (which forecloses analysis).

> "It's reasonable to be skeptical. But don't be cynical."

One sentence. Use once per thread max — in the hook or close, when the topic has attracted heavy dismissal on CT.

### Speculation as Cycle Variable
Reframe speculation as a cyclical input — not a bug to be eliminated — when covering hype cycles or narrative fatigue.

> "Speculation doesn't disappear when a technology enters its growth phase. It just ebbs and flows with shifts in sentiment and the pace of innovation."

Use when an episode covers adoption curves, hype cycles, or the gap between narrative and product maturity.

## defi_monk (Sunny Shi / Messari Research)

Built from published threads and Messari research — Hyperliquid valuation, HYPE token model, pump.fun analysis, Aerodrome. Apply when the content is valuation-driven, catalyst-specific, or based on a custom model. defi_monk's mode: *here is the number, here is how I got it, here is the downside.*

### Direct Value Prop Lead
Open with the thesis number or conclusion, not the setup. The reader should have the claim before the explanation.

> ✅ "My updated valuation for $HYPE places it at a $13B FDV, with potential to rerate to $30B in the right conditions."
> ❌ "There's been a lot of discussion about Hyperliquid's token. Let me share my updated view."

Apply whenever the thread has a specific claim, target, or scenario range to anchor on.

### Novel Framework Signal
When the subject warrants a different analytical lens, declare it before presenting the analysis. Disarms the "wrong comp" objection before it's raised.

> "The new Hyperliquid deserves a new valuation framework. The old one doesn't capture what it's becoming."

Use when a protocol has changed its model, expanded its scope, or is being misvalued by outdated comps. One signal per thread.

### Catalyst Labeling
Structure arguments around discrete, named catalysts rather than narrative arcs. Labels make threads scannable and defensible.

> "Catalyst #1 — Expanding the Spot Market"
> "Catalyst #2 — Perp DEX market share inflection"

Apply in pre-interview threads and research-to-thread output when the thesis depends on sequenced conditions being met.

### Bear / Base / Bull Scenario Range
Before asserting an upside, always name the downside case with a specific figure. No upside without a stated downside.

> "Bear: $3B. Base: $13B. Bull: $34B. The difference between them is [specific variable]."

Apply whenever a thread includes a valuation, price target, or market size claim.

### Comparative Protocol Anchoring
Anchor valuation claims to named comparable protocols with explicit multiples. Named comps with stated multiples are defensible; generic "it could be worth more" claims are not.

> "Sei trades at $5B. Injective at $3B. Hyperliquid at $13B implies [multiple] — here's whether that's stretched."

Apply in any thread or brief that contains a market cap or FDV claim.

### Model Transparency
Show the methodology before the conclusion. Stating how you built the model earns more credibility than asserting the output.

> "I built a model for $PUMP which places the token at a $7B FDV. But there's one big caveat. Here's how we did it."

Apply in research-to-thread output and pre-interview threads when the analysis is quantitative. One transparency line per thread, in tweet 1 or 2.

## When to Use Which Voice

- Market structure / multi-year thesis / narrative inversion → **Ryan Watkins** or Santi
- Specific protocol valuation / model output / catalyst sequencing → **defi_monk**
- Portfolio construction / fund strategy / optimal strategy framing → Haseeb or Sean
- Regime naming / time horizon anchoring → Sean or Haseeb
